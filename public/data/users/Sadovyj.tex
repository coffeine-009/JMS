\section*{Introduction}

A thick junction of type $m:k:d$ is a union of some domain, which is called the junction's body, and a large number of $\varepsilon$-periodically alternating thin domains, which are attached to some manifold (the joint zone) on the boundary of the junction's body. The small parameter $\varepsilon$ characterizes  distance between  neighboring thin domains and their thickness. The type $m:k:d$ of a thick junction refers, respectively, to the limiting dimensions (as $\varepsilon \to 0$) of the junction's body, the joint zone and each of the attached thin domains.

The subject of the investigation of boundary-value problems in thick junctions is the asymptotic behavior of solutions to such problems as $\varepsilon \to 0$, i.e. when the number of the attached thin domains infinitely increases and their thickness tends to zero.

The first researches in this direction were carried out in
\cite{Khrus,Kot-Kh,MarKhrus}, where the convergence theorems for Green function
of the Neumann problem for the Helmholz equation in the junction's body were
proved. In these papers either the assumption about the convergence of certain
components of the boundary-value problem was made, or explicit representations
of certain quantities were used, which was possible under certain configurations
of the junction's body (the half-space). In \cite{MN93d}--\cite{Mel'nyk-1},
\cite{N95} thick junctions were classified, asymptotic methods for the
investigation
of main boundary-value problems of mathematical physics in thick junctions
of different types were developed, the convergence theorems were proved, the first
terms of asymptotic expansions were constructed, and the corresponding estimates
were proved. It was shown that qualitative properties of solutions essentially depend on the junction's type and
the conditions given on the boundaries of the attached thin domains (see also  \cite{B-G-M,B-Gaudiello-Gri,MC3}).

As an extension of the investigation, in papers \cite{MDM,D-M,M-V-2} thick junctions of more complicated geometric structure were considered, namely multi-level thick junctions. A multi-level thick junction is a thick junction, in which thin domains are divided into finitely many levels depending on their geometric structure and boundary conditions imposed on their surfaces. Besides, thin domains from each level
$\varepsilon$-periodically alternate along the joint zone.
In these papers linear boundary-value problems in thick junctions of types $2:1:1$ and $3:2:1$
were considered. Moreover, there a new qualitative difference in the asymptotic behavior of solutions to boundary-value problems in multi-level thick junctions was noticed, namely the "multi-phase" \ effect in the domain that is filled up simultaneously by the thin domains from different levels.

The successful applying in nanotechnology and microelectronics  of
constructions, which have form of thick junctions (see Fig.~1 and
\cite{Lav-Kn-Pid}--\cite{Lyshevski}), has lead to effective studying of
boundary-value problems in thick junctions of various types and more complicated
structure (see also \cite{B-G-M}--\cite{AppAn}, \cite{Mel-m2as-08,MC3}).

In the present paper we consider quasilinear parabolic boundary-value problem in a two-level thick junction of type $3:2:2$, which consists of a cylinder $\Omega_0$ and a large number of thin annular discs with varying thickness, which are $\varepsilon$-periodically attached to $\Omega_0$. Different nonhomogeneous Robin boundary conditions are given on the surfaces of the thin discs from various levels. The leading terms of the asymptotic expansion for a solution to this problem are constructed and the asymptotic estimate in Sobolev space is proved.

The outline of the paper is as follows. In Section \ref{STATEMENT_ELLIPTIC} thick junction $\Omega_\varepsilon$ is described and quasilinear elliptic boundary-value problem in this thick junction is stated. In Section \ref{SECTION_FORMAL} outer and inner asymptotic expansions for the solution $u_\varepsilon$ are constructed and homogenized boundary-value problem is obtained. In Section \ref{SECTION_ASYMPT_ESTIM} approximation function $R_\varepsilon$ for solution $u_\varepsilon$ is constructed and asymptotic estimate is proved. In Section \ref{SECTION_DISCUSS} the obtained results are discussed.

\section{Statement of Problem}\label{STATEMENT_ELLIPTIC}

Let $0< d_0 < d_2 \le d_1$ and   $0< b_2 < b_1 <1$; $h_i:
[d_0, \, d_i] \rightarrow (0, \, 1), \ i=1, \, 2,$ are piecewise smooth functions. Suppose that  functions $h_i$ satisfy the following conditions:
$$
0<b_i-\frac{h_i(s)}{2}, \  b_i+\frac{h_i(s)}{2}<1 \quad \forall s \in [d_0,\,d_i], \ i=1, \, 2, \quad
b_2+\frac{h_2(s)}{2}< b_1-\frac{h_1(s)}{2} \quad \forall s \in
[d_0, \, d_2].
$$
These inequalities imply that for all $s \in [d_0, \, d_i]$ the intervals
$$
I_i(s):=\left(b_i-\frac{h_i(s)}{2}, \, b_i+\frac{h_i(s)}{2}\right), \quad i=1,\,2,
$$
belong to interval $(0, \, 1)$, don't have common points and don't adjoin.

We additionally assume that  functions $h_1, \, h_2$ are constant in some neighborhood of $d_0$, i.e. there exists $\delta >0$ such that $h_i(s) = h_i(d_0)$ for all $s \in [d_0, \, d_0 + \delta], \ i=1, \, 2$.

Consider a model thick junction $\Omega_{\varepsilon}$ of type $3:2:2$ (see Fig.~2) that consists of cylinder
$$
\Omega_0 = \{ x = (x_1, \, x_2, \, x_3) \in \mathbb{R}^3 : \ \, 0< x_2 <l,
\ \, r:= \sqrt{x_1^2 + x_3^2} < d_0 \}
$$
and $2 N$ thin annular discs
$$
G_\varepsilon^{(1)}(j)=\{ x \in \mathbb{R}^3: \ \, |x_2- \varepsilon (j+b_1)|<\frac{\varepsilon h_1 (r)}{2}, \ \, d_0 \leq r <d_1 \},
$$
$$
G_\varepsilon^{(2)}(j)=\{ x \in \mathbb{R}^3: \ \, |x_2- \varepsilon (j+b_2)|<\frac{\varepsilon h_2 (r)}{2}, \ \, d_0 \leq r <d_2 \},
$$
where $j= \overline{0, \, N-1}, \ \varepsilon = l/N,$ i.e.
$$
\Omega_{\varepsilon}=\Omega_0 \cup G_{\varepsilon}, \quad
G_{\varepsilon}=G^{(1)}_{\varepsilon} \cup
G^{(2)}_{\varepsilon}, \quad G^{(1)}_{\varepsilon}=
\cup_{j=0}^{N-1} G_\varepsilon^{(1)}(j), \quad G^{(2)}_{\varepsilon}= \cup_{j=0}^{N-1}
G_\varepsilon^{(2)}(j).
$$
Here $N$ is a large integer. Therefore, $\varepsilon$
is a small parameter, which characterizes  distance between  neighboring thin discs and their thickness.

 Denote by $S_\varepsilon^{(1)}$ and $S_\varepsilon^{(2)}$ the union of the lateral surfaces of the thin discs from the first and the second level, respectively, and by $S^\pm$ the bases of  cylinder $\Omega_0$, i.e.
\begin{gather*}
S_\varepsilon^{(i)} := \{ x \in \partial G_\varepsilon^{(i)} : \ \, |x_2 - \varepsilon (j+b_i)| = \varepsilon  h_i(r) / 2, \ j = \overline{0, N-1}, \ r \in (d_0, \, d_i)\}, \quad i=1, \, 2,
\\[2mm]
S^- = \{ x \in \partial \Omega_0 : \ x_2=0\},
\quad S^+ = \{ x \in \partial \Omega_0 : \ x_2=l\}, \quad S^\pm=S^+ \cup S^-.
\end{gather*}

Also we introduce the following notations:
\begin{gather*}
\overline{\Omega}_i=\overline{\Omega}_0 \cup \overline{D}_i,
\quad D_i=\{x \in \mathbb{R}^3 : \ 0<x_2<l, \ d_0 < r < d_i \}, \ i=1, \, 2,
\\
Q^{(i)}_0=\{ x \in \partial \Omega_i : \
r=d_i\}, \ i=\overline{0,2}, \quad
Q^{(i)}_{\varepsilon}= \{ x \in \partial G_{\varepsilon}^{(i)} : \ r=d_i\}, \ i=1, \, 2,
\\
\Upsilon_\varepsilon^{(i)} = S_\varepsilon^{(i)} \cup Q_\varepsilon^{(i)}, \quad \Theta^{(i)}_{\varepsilon} = G^{(i)}_{\varepsilon} \cap \partial\Omega_0 , \ i=1, \, 2, \quad \Theta_\varepsilon = \Theta_\varepsilon^{(1)} \cup  \Theta_\varepsilon^{(2)}, \quad Q_\varepsilon^{(0)} = Q_0^{(0)} \setminus \Theta_\varepsilon.
\end{gather*}

In thick junction $\Omega_\varepsilon$ we consider the quasilinear elliptic boundary-value problem
\begin{equation}
\label{task1}\left\{
        \begin{array}{rcll}
    - \Delta u_{\varepsilon} + \vartheta_0(u_\varepsilon) &=& f_{\varepsilon}
                    &\mbox{in} \ \Omega_{\varepsilon}  , \\[2mm]
    \partial_{\nu} u_{\varepsilon} + \varepsilon   \vartheta_1(u_\varepsilon) &=& \varepsilon^{\beta} g_{\varepsilon}
                    &\mbox{on} \ S_{\varepsilon}^{(1)} ,
    \\[2mm]
    \partial_{\nu} u_{\varepsilon} +  \vartheta_1(u_\varepsilon) &=& 0
                    &\mbox{on} \ Q_{\varepsilon}^{(1)} ,
    \\[2mm]
    \partial_{\nu} u_{\varepsilon} + \varepsilon^\alpha   \vartheta_2(u_\varepsilon) &=& \varepsilon^{\beta} g_{\varepsilon}
                    &\mbox{on} \ \Upsilon_{\varepsilon}^{(2)} ,
    \\[2mm]
    \partial_{\nu} u_\varepsilon &=& 0\
                    &\mbox{on} \ Q_\varepsilon^{(0)},
    \\[2mm]
    \partial_{x_2}^p u_{\varepsilon}|_{S^-} &=&     \partial_{x_2}^p u_{\varepsilon}|_{S^+},
                    &p=0, \, 1,
    \\[2mm]
        \left. [u_\varepsilon] \right|_{r=d_0}
    =\left. [\partial_r u_\varepsilon] \right|_{r=d_0} &=&0
                    &\mbox{on} \ \Theta_\varepsilon.
   \end{array}\right.
\end{equation}
Here $\partial_{\nu}=\partial/\partial \nu$ is the outward normal derivative; $\alpha, \ \beta \geq 1$ are parameters;
 the square brackets denote the jump of the enclosed quantities. For the right-hand sides of problem (\ref{task1}) we assume that $f_\varepsilon \in L^2(\Omega_\varepsilon)$, $g_\varepsilon \in H^1(D_1)$ and
$$
\exists C_0 > 0 \quad \exists \varepsilon_0>0 \quad \forall \varepsilon \in (0, \, \varepsilon_0): \quad \| g_\varepsilon\|_{L^2(D_1)} + \|\partial_{x_2} g_\varepsilon\|_{L^2(D_1)}\le C_0.
$$
Functions $\vartheta_i$ are Lipschitz-continuous (which is equal to $\vartheta_i \in W^{1,\infty}_{\rm loc}(\mathbb{R})$) and
\begin{equation}\label{nonlin_condin}
\exists c_1, \, c_2 >0: \quad c_1 \le \vartheta_i'(s) \le c_2 \quad  \mbox{for a.e. }  s \in \mathbb{R}, \ i=\overline{0,\, 2}.
\end{equation}

Consider spaces $H_\varepsilon = \{ \varphi \in H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon): \ \varphi|_{S^-} = \varphi|_{S^+} \}$.

A function $u_\varepsilon \in H_\varepsilon$ is a weak solution to problem (\ref{task1}) if for any function $\varphi \in
H_\varepsilon$ the following integral identity holds:
\begin{multline}\label{weakdef}
\int_{\Omega_{\varepsilon}} (\nabla u_{\varepsilon}\cdot
\nabla \varphi + \vartheta_0(u_\varepsilon) \, \varphi)\, dx +\varepsilon
 \int_{S^{(1)}_{\varepsilon}} \vartheta_1(u_{\varepsilon}) \,
    \varphi \,  d\sigma_x + \int_{Q_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \vartheta_1(u_\varepsilon) \, \varphi \, d\sigma_x
\\
+\varepsilon^{\alpha}  \int_{\Upsilon^{(2)}_{\varepsilon}} \vartheta_2(u_{\varepsilon}) \, \varphi \, d\sigma_x
= \int_{\Omega_{\varepsilon}} f_{\varepsilon} \,
    \varphi \, dx
+\varepsilon^{\beta} \int_{S^{(1)}_{\varepsilon}\cup
    \Upsilon^{(2)}_{\varepsilon}}
    g_{\varepsilon} \, \varphi \, d\sigma_x.
\end{multline}

By the same arguments as in \cite{Mel-m2as-08} we  can prove that for any fixed $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists a unique weak solution to problem~(\ref{task1}).

\textsf{The aim is to study the asymptotic behavior of the  solution to problem (\ref{task1}) as $\varepsilon \to 0$, i.e. when the number of the attached thin discs infinitely increases and their thickness tends to zero.}

\section{Formal Asymptotic Expansions for the Solution}\label{SECTION_FORMAL}

Only in this Section for formal calculations we assume that  functions $f_\varepsilon, \, g_\varepsilon$ do not depend on $\varepsilon$, i.e.  $f_\varepsilon = f_0$ in $\Omega_1$ and $g_\varepsilon = g_0$ in $D_1$, and they are smooth in $\overline \Omega_1 $ and $\overline D_1$, respectively.

\subsection{Outer Expansions}

We seek the leading terms of the asymptotic expansion for solution $u_\varepsilon$, restricted to $\Omega_0$, in the form
\begin{equation}\label{exp_out_cyl}
u_\varepsilon(x) \approx  u_0^+(x) + \sum_{k\ge1} \varepsilon^k  u_k^+ (x), \quad x \in \Omega_0,
\end{equation}
and, restricted to the thin discs $G^{(i)}_\varepsilon(j), \ j=\overline{0 , N-1},$ in the form
\begin{equation}\label{exp_out_discs}
u_\varepsilon(x) \approx u_0^{i,-}(x) + \sum_{k \ge 1} \varepsilon^k u_k^{i,-}(x, \, \xi_2 - j), \quad x \in G_\varepsilon^{(i)}(j), \quad i=1,\,2,
\end{equation}
where $\xi_2 = x_2 / \varepsilon$.

Expansions (\ref{exp_out_cyl}) and (\ref{exp_out_discs}) are usually called \textsl{outer expansions}.

With the help of Taylor's formula we get
\begin{equation}\label{Taylor+}
\vartheta_0(u_\varepsilon(x)) = \vartheta_0 (u_0^+(x))  + \vartheta_0'(\, \cdot \, ) \sum_{k\ge1} \varepsilon^k  u_k^+ (x), \quad x \in \Omega_0.
\end{equation}

Plugging the series (\ref{exp_out_cyl}) into the first equation of problem (\ref{task1}) and the boundary conditions on $S^\pm$, using (\ref{Taylor+}) and collecting coefficients of the same powers of $\varepsilon$, we get the following relations for   function $u_0^+$:
$$
\left\{
\begin{array}{rcll}
- \Delta u_0^+  + \vartheta_0(u_0^+) &=& f_0 & \mbox{in} \  \Omega_0, \\[2mm]
\partial_{x_2}^p u_0^+|_{S^-} &=& \partial_{x_2}^p u_0^+|_{S^+}, & p=0, \, 1.
\end{array}
\right.
$$

Now let us find the limit relations in   domains $D_i, \ i=1,\,2$, which are filled up by the thin discs from $i$-th level as $\varepsilon$ tends to zero. Assuming for a moment that   functions $u_k^{i,-}$ are smooth, we write their Taylor series with respect to $x_2$ at the point $\varepsilon (j + b_i)$ and pass to the "rapid" \ variable $\xi_2 = x_2 / \varepsilon.$ Then (\ref{exp_out_discs}) takes the form
\begin{equation}\label{exp_out_fast}
u_\varepsilon(x) \approx u_0^{i,-}(x_1, \, \varepsilon  (j+b_i), \, x_3) + \sum_{k\ge1} \varepsilon^k  V_k^{i,j}(\tilde{x}, \, \xi_2), \quad x \in G^{(i)}_\varepsilon(j),
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{x}:=(x_1, \, x_3)$, and
\begin{multline}\label{V}
V_k^{i,j}(\tilde{x}, \, \xi_2) = \sum_{m=0}^{k-1} \frac{(\xi_2 - j - b_i)^m}{m!}
\frac{\partial^m u_{k-m}^{i,-}}{\partial x_2^m} (x_1, \, \varepsilon  (j+b_i), \, x_3, \, \xi_2 - j)
\\
+ \frac{(\xi_2 - j - b_i)^k}{k!} \frac{\partial^k u_0^{i,-}}{\partial x_2^k} (x_1, \, \varepsilon  (j+b_i), \, x_3).
\end{multline}

Further we will indicate   arguments of functions only if their absence may cause confusion.

The outward unit normal to the lateral surfaces of the thin discs except a set of zero measure is as follows:
\begin{equation}\label{normal}
\nu_\varepsilon (x)=\frac{1}{ \sqrt{ 1 + 4^{-1} \varepsilon^2
|h_i'(r)|^2 }} \left( - \frac {\varepsilon h_i'(r) x_1}{2 r}, \, \pm
1 , \, - \frac{\varepsilon h_i'(r) x_3} {2 r} \right), \quad x \in S_\varepsilon^{(i)}, \ i=1,\,2,
\end{equation}
where "$+$" \  and "$-$" \  refer, respectively, to the left and the right parts of the lateral surface of each thin disc. Obviously, $({ 1 + \varepsilon^2 4^{-1} |h_i'(r)|^2 })^{-\frac{1}{2}} = 1 + {\cal O}(\varepsilon^2), \ \varepsilon \to 0.$

Again with the help of Taylor's formula we obtain
\begin{equation}\label{Taylor-}
\vartheta_0(u_\varepsilon(x)) = \vartheta_0 (u_0^{i,-}(x_1, \, \varepsilon(j+b_i), \, x_3)) + \vartheta_0'(\, \cdot \, ) \sum_{k\ge1} \varepsilon^k  V_k^{i,j}(\tilde{x}, \, \xi_2, \, t), \ x \in G_\varepsilon^{(i)}.
\end{equation}

Let us put (\ref{exp_out_fast}) into (\ref{task1}) instead of $u_\varepsilon$. Taking into account (\ref{normal}), (\ref{Taylor-}) and that the Laplace operator in the variables $(\tilde{x}, \, \xi_2)$ has the form $\Delta_x = \Delta_{\tilde{x}} + \varepsilon^{-2} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \xi_2^2}$ and collecting   coefficients of the same powers of $\varepsilon$, we arrive at one-dimensional boundary-value problems with respect to $\xi_2$ for functions $V_k^{i,j}$.

Problems for $V_1^{i,j}$ read
\begin{equation}\label{hom_sec}
\left\{
\begin{array}{rcll}
\partial^2_{\xi_2\xi_2} V_1^{i,j} &=& 0, & \xi_2 \in I_{h_i(r)}(j) := ( - \frac{h_i(r)}{2} + j+ b_i , \, \frac{h_i(r)}{2} + j + b_i),\\[2mm]
\partial_{\xi_2} V_1^{i,j} &=& 0, & \xi_2 = \pm \frac{h_i(r)}{2} + j + b_i,
\end{array}
\right. \qquad (i=1, \, 2)
\end{equation}
where $\partial_{\xi_2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial\xi_2}, \ \partial^2_{\xi_2\xi_2} = \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \xi_2^2}.$ Here the variables $\tilde{x}$ are regarded as parameters.

It follows from (\ref{hom_sec}) that $V_1^{i,j}$ do  not depend on $\xi_2$. Therefore, $V_1^{i,j}$ are equal to some functions $\varphi^{(i)}_1 (x_1, \, \varepsilon (j+b_i), \, x_3), \ x \in G_\varepsilon^{(i)}(j)$, which will be defined later. Then, due to (\ref{V}) we have
\begin{multline}\label{with_phi}
u_1^{i,-}(x_1, \, \varepsilon (j+b_i), \, x_3, \, \xi_2 - j) = \varphi^{(i)}_1 (x_1, \, \varepsilon (j+b_i), \, x_3) \\ - (\xi_2 -j -b_i) \partial_{x_2} u_0^{i,-} (x_1, \, \varepsilon (j+b_i), \, x_3), \quad x \in G_\varepsilon^{(i)}(j).
\end{multline}

Boundary-value problems for $V_2^{1,j}$ and $V_2^{2,j}$ have the view
\begin{equation}\label{hom_V^1}
\left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
-\partial^2_{\xi_2\xi_2} V_2^{1,j} = ( \Delta_{\tilde{x}} u_0^{1,-} - \vartheta_0(u_0^{1,-}) + f_0)|_{x_2 = \varepsilon (j+b_1)}, & \xi_2 \in I_{h_1(r)}(j),\\[2mm]
\pm \partial_{\xi_2} V_2^{1,j} = (2^{-1} \nabla_{\tilde{x}} h_1 \cdot \nabla_{\tilde{x}} u_0^{1,-} - \vartheta_1 (u_0^{1,-}) + \delta_{\beta,1} g_0) |_{x_2 = \varepsilon (j+b_1)}, & \xi_2 = \pm \frac{h_1(r)}{2} + j +  b_1,
\end{array}
\right.
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{hom_V^2}
\left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
-\partial^2_{\xi_2\xi_2} V_2^{2,j} = (\Delta_{\tilde{x}} u_0^{2,-} - \vartheta_0(u_0^{2,-}) + f_0)|_{x_2 = \varepsilon (j+b_2)}, & \xi_2 \in I_{h_2(r)}(j),\\[2mm]
\pm \partial_{\xi_2} V_2^{2,j} = (2^{-1} \nabla_{\tilde{x}} h_2 \cdot \nabla_{\tilde{x}} u_0^{2,-} -  \delta_{\alpha,1} \vartheta_2(u_0^{2,-}) + \delta_{\beta,1} g_0)|_{x_2 = \varepsilon (j+b_2)}, & \xi_2 = \pm \frac{h_2(r)}{2}  + j + b_2,
\end{array}
\right.
\end{equation}
respectively, where $\delta_{\alpha,1}, \, \delta_{\beta,1}$ are Kronecker's symbols.

The solvability conditions for problems (\ref{hom_V^1}) and (\ref{hom_V^2}) read
\begin{multline}\label{inD1}
- \mbox{div}_{\tilde{x}} (h_1 \nabla_{\tilde{x}} u_0^{1,-}) + h_1 \vartheta_0(u_0^{1,-})
    + 2 \vartheta_1 (u_0^{1,-}) = h_1 f_0 + 2 \delta_{\beta,1}  g_0,\\  x_2 = \varepsilon (j+b_1), \ r \in (d_0, \, d_1),
\end{multline}
\begin{multline}\label{inD2}
-\mbox{div}_{\tilde{x}} (h_2 \nabla_{\tilde{x}} u_0^{2,-}) + h_2 \vartheta_0(u_0^{2,-}) + 2 \delta_{\alpha,1} \vartheta_2 (u_0^{2,-})  = h_2 f_0 + 2 \delta_{\beta,1} g_0, \\ x_2 = \varepsilon (j+b_2), \ r \in (d_0, \, d_2),
\end{multline}
respectively.

Putting (\ref{exp_out_fast}) into the Robin boundary conditions on $Q_\varepsilon^{(i)},$ we get
\begin{eqnarray}\label{inQ1}
\partial_r u_0^{1,-} + \vartheta_1 ( u_0^{1,-})& = &0, \quad x \in Q_\varepsilon^{(1)}, \ x_2 = \varepsilon(j+b_1),\\
\label{inQ2}
\partial_r u_0^{2,-} &= &0,  \quad x \in Q_\varepsilon^{(2)}, \ x_2 = \varepsilon(j+b_2).
\end{eqnarray}

In order to find   conditions in   joint zone $Q_0^{(0)}$ we use the method of matched asymptotic expansions for   outer expansions (\ref{exp_out_cyl}), (\ref{exp_out_fast}) and an inner expansion which will be constructed in the next subsection.

\subsection{Inner Expansion}\label{SECTION_JUNCTION_LAYER}

In a neighborhood of   joint zone $Q_0^{(0)}$ we introduce the "rapid" \ coordinates $\xi=(\xi_1, \, \xi_2)$, where $\xi_1 = - (r- d_0) / \varepsilon$ and $\xi_2=x_2 / \varepsilon.$ Here $(r, \, x_2, \, \theta) \in \mathbb{R}^3$ are cylindric coordinates: $r=\sqrt{x_1^2 + x_3^2}, \ \tan(\theta)=x_3/x_1$.
The Laplace operator in the coordinates $(\xi_1, \, \xi_2, \, \theta)$ has the form
\begin{equation}\label{Laplace}
\Delta_x = \varepsilon^{-2} \Delta_\xi - \varepsilon^{-1} \frac{1}{d_0- \varepsilon  \xi_1} \frac{\partial}{\partial \xi_1} + \frac{1}{(d_0 - \varepsilon \xi_1)^2} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \theta^2}.
\end{equation}

We seek the leading terms of the inner expansion in a neighborhood of $Q_0^{(0)}$ in the form
\begin{multline}\label{exp_in}
u_\varepsilon(x) \approx u_0^+(x)|_{r=d_0} + \varepsilon \big( Z_1(\xi) \partial_{x_2} u_0^+(x)|_{r=d_0} \\ - \big(\eta(x_2) \Xi_1(\xi) + (1-\eta(x_2)) \Xi_2(\xi)\big) \partial_r u_0^+(x)|_{r=d_0}\big) + \ldots,
\end{multline}
where $Z_1, \ \Xi_1, \ \Xi_2$ are some functions, which are 1-periodic with respect to $\xi_2$ and defined in the union $\Pi:=\Pi^+ \cup \Pi^-_1 \cup \Pi^-_2$ of semiinfinite strips
$$
\Pi^+ = \{ \xi \in \mathbb{R}^2: \ \, \xi_1 > 0, \ \xi_2 \in (0, \, 1)\},
\qquad
\Pi^-_i = \{ \xi \in \mathbb{R}^2: \ \, \xi_1 \le 0, \ \xi_2 \in I_i(d_0) \}, \quad i=1, \, 2,
$$
(see definition of $I_i(d_0)$ in Section \ref{STATEMENT_ELLIPTIC}), $\eta $ is some function, which will be defined from matching conditions.

Putting (\ref{exp_in}) into the differential equation of problem (\ref{task1}) with regard to (\ref{Laplace}) and into the corresponding boundary conditions and collecting coefficients of the same powers of $\varepsilon$, we get the junction-layer problems for  functions $Z_1, \ \Xi_1, \ \Xi_2.$ Functions $\Xi_1$ and $\Xi_2$ are  solutions to the following homogeneous problem:
\begin{equation}\label{junction-layer_Xi}
\left\{
\begin{array}{rcll}
-\Delta_\xi \Xi &=&0 &\mbox{in } \Pi, \\[2mm]
\partial_{\xi_2} \Xi &=&0 &\mbox{on } (\partial \Pi^-_1 \cup \partial \Pi^-_2) \cap \{ \xi \in \mathbb{R}^2: \ \xi_1 < 0\}, \\[2mm]
\partial_{\xi_1} \Xi &=&0 &\mbox{on } \partial \Pi \cap \{ \xi \in \mathbb{R}^2 : \ \xi_1 = 0\}, \\[2mm]
\partial_{\xi_2}^p \Xi|_{\xi_2 =0} &=& \partial_{\xi_2}^p \Xi|_{\xi_2=1}, &p=0, \, 1, \ \xi_1 > 0.
\end{array}
\right.
\end{equation}

Main asymptotic relations for   functions $\Xi_1, \ \Xi_2$ can be obtained from general results on the asymptotic behavior of solutions to elliptic problems in domains with different exits to infinity (see, for instance, \cite{Nazarov-Plamen}). However, for  domain $\Pi$, we can define more exactly the asymptotic relations for  junction-layer solutions $\Xi_1, \ \Xi_2$ in the same way as in papers \cite{MN96,M99zaa}.

\begin{proposition} There exist two solutions $\Xi_1, \ \Xi_2 \in H^1_{\sharp, \rm loc}(\Pi)$ to problem (\ref{junction-layer_Xi}), which have the following differentiable asymptotics:
\begin{eqnarray}\label{xi_asymt_1}
\Xi_1 = \left\{
    \begin{array}{ll}
    \xi_1 + {\cal O}(\exp(-2  \pi \xi_1)), & \xi_1 \to +\infty, \ \xi \in \Pi^+,\\
    \alpha_1^{(1)} + {\cal O} (\exp(\pi  h_1^{-1}(d_0)  \xi_1)), & \xi_1 \to - \infty, \ \xi \in \Pi^-_1,\\
    h_2^{-1}(d_0)  \xi_1 + \alpha_1^{(2)} + {\cal O}(\exp(\pi  h_2^{-1}(d_0)  \xi_1)), & \xi_1 \to - \infty, \ \xi \in \Pi^-_2,
    \end{array}
    \right. \\ \label{xi_asymt_2}
\Xi_2 = \left\{
    \begin{array}{ll}
    \xi_1 + {\cal O}(\exp(-2 \pi \xi_1)), & \xi_1 \to +\infty, \ \xi \in \Pi^+,\\
    h_1^{-1}(d_0)  \xi_1 + \alpha_2^{(1)} + {\cal O}(\exp(\pi   h_1^{-1}(d_0)  \xi_1)), & \xi_1 \to - \infty, \ \xi \in \Pi^-_1,\\
    \alpha_2^{(2)} + {\cal O} (\exp(\pi  h_2^{-1}(d_0)  \xi_1)), & \xi_1 \to - \infty, \ \xi \in \Pi^-_2.
    \end{array}
    \right.
\end{eqnarray}
Here $H^1_{\sharp,\rm loc}(\Pi) = \{ u: \Pi \to \mathbb{R} \, :  \ \,  u(\xi_1, \, 0) = u(\xi_1, \, 1) \ \mbox{for any} \ \xi_1>0, \ u\in H^1(\Pi_R) \ \mbox{for any}$ $R>0\}$, $\Pi_R = \{\xi \in \Pi: \ \, -R < \xi_1 < R\}$; $\alpha_1^{(i)}, \ \alpha_2^{(i)}, \ i=1, \, 2,$ are some fixed constants.

Any other solution to problem (\ref{junction-layer_Xi}), which has a polynomial growth at infinity, can be represented as a linear combination $c_0 + c_1 \Xi_1 + c_2 \Xi_2$.
\end{proposition}

Function $Z_1$ is a solution to the following problem:
\begin{equation}\label{junction-layer_Z}
\left\{
\begin{array}{rcll}
-\Delta_\xi Z &=&0 &\mbox{in } \Pi, \\[2mm]
\partial_{\xi_2} Z &=&-1 &\mbox{on } (\partial \Pi^-_1 \cup \partial \Pi^-_2) \cap \{ \xi \in \mathbb{R}^2: \ \xi_1 < 0\},\\[2mm]
\partial_{\xi_1} Z &=&0 &\mbox{on } \partial \Pi \cap \{ \xi \in \mathbb{R}^2: \ \xi_1 = 0\}, \\[2mm]
\partial_{\xi_2}^p Z|_{\xi_2=0} &=& \partial_{\xi_2}^p Z|_{\xi_2=1}, &p=0, \, 1, \ \xi_1 > 0.
\end{array}
\right.
\end{equation}

Similarly to \cite{MN96,M99zaa,Nazarov} it is easy to verify that there exists a unique solution $Z_1 \in H^1_{\sharp,\rm loc}(\Pi)$ with the following asymptotics:
\begin{equation}\label{z_asympt}
Z = \left\{
    \begin{array}{ll}
    {\cal O}(\exp(-2  \pi \xi_1)), & \xi_1 \to +\infty, \ \xi \in \Pi^+,\\
    - \xi_2 + b_1 + \alpha_3^{(1)} + {\cal O} (\exp(\pi  h_1^{-1}(d_0)  \xi_1)), & \xi_1 \to - \infty, \ \xi \in \Pi^-_1,\\
    - \xi_2 + b_2 + \alpha_3^{(2)} + {\cal O} (\exp(\pi  h_2^{-1}(d_0)  \xi_1)), & \xi_1 \to - \infty, \ \xi \in \Pi^-_2.
    \end{array}
    \right.
\end{equation}

Now let us verify matching conditions for   outer expansions (\ref{exp_out_cyl}), (\ref{exp_out_discs}) and   inner expansion (\ref{exp_in}), namely, the leading terms of the asymptotics of the outer expansions as $\xi_1 \to \pm0 $ must coincide with the leading terms of the asymptotics of the inner expansion as $\xi_1 \to \pm\infty$. Near the point $(x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_i), \, x_3) \in Q_0^{(0)}$ function $u_0^+$ has the following asymptotics:
\begin{multline*}
u_0^+(x) \approx u_0^+(x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_i), \, x_3)|_{r=d_0}
 + \varepsilon(\xi_2 - j -b_i) \partial_{x_2} u_0^+(x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_i), \, x_3)|_{r=d_0}
\\ - \varepsilon \xi_1  \partial_r u_0^+(x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_i), \, x_3)|_{r=d_0} + \ldots\quad \mbox{as} \ \xi_1 \to 0+, \ (x) \in \Omega_0.
\end{multline*}
Taking into account the asymptotics of $Z_1, \ \Xi_1$ and $\Xi_2$ as $\xi_1\to+\infty$, we see that the matching conditions are satisfied for   expansions (\ref{exp_out_cyl}) and (\ref{exp_in}).

The asymptotics of (\ref{exp_out_discs}) in the neighborhood of $(x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_i), \, x_3) \in Q_0^{(0)}$ are equal to
\begin{multline}\label{match_discs}
u_0^{i,-}(x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_i), \, x_3 )|_{r=d_0} + \varepsilon  \big(\varphi^{(i)}_1(x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_i), \, x_3 )|_{r=d_0}
\\
 - \xi_1 \partial_r u_0^{i,-}(x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_i), \, x_3 )|_{r=d_0} \big)  + \ldots  \quad \mbox{as} \ \xi_1 \to 0-, \ x \in G_\varepsilon^{(i)}(j), \  i=1, \, 2.
\end{multline}
It follows from (\ref{xi_asymt_1}), (\ref{xi_asymt_2}) and (\ref{z_asympt}) that the first terms of the asymptotics of (\ref{exp_in}) in the neighborhood of $(x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_i), \, x_3) \in Q_0^{(0)}$ are
\begin{multline}\label{match_in_1}
u_0^+(x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_1), \, x_3)|_{r=d_0} + \varepsilon \big( \alpha_3^{(1)} \partial_{x_2} u_0^+(x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_1), \, x_3)|_{r=d_0}
\\
 -\big(\alpha_1^{(1)} \eta(\varepsilon (j + b_1)) + (h_1^{-1}(d_0) \xi_1 + \alpha_2^{(1)}) (1- \eta(\varepsilon( j + b_1)) )\big) \partial_r u_0^+ (x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_1), \, x_3)|_{r=d_0} \big) \\ \mbox{as} \ \xi_1 \to -\infty, \ x \in G_\varepsilon^{(1)}(j),
\end{multline}
and
\begin{multline}\label{match_in_2}
u_0^+(x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_2), \, x_3)|_{r=d_0} + \varepsilon \, \big( \alpha_3^{(2)} \partial_{x_2} u_0^+ (x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_2), \, x_3)|_{r=d_0}
\\
 -\big((h_2^{-1}(d_0) \xi_1 + \alpha_1^{(2)})  \eta(\varepsilon( j + b_2) ) + \alpha_2^{(2)}  (1- \eta(\varepsilon ( j + b_2)  )) \big) \partial_r u_0^+ (x_1, \, \varepsilon( j + b_2), \, x_3 )|_{r=d_0} \big) \\ \mbox{as} \ \xi_1 \to -\infty, \ x \in G_\varepsilon^{(2)}(j).
\end{multline}

Comparing the first terms of (\ref{match_discs}), (\ref{match_in_1}) and (\ref{match_in_2}), we get
\begin{equation}\label{conjugation1}
u_0^+(x) = u_0^{i,-}(x), \quad x \in Q_0^{(0)}, \ x_2 = \varepsilon(j + b_i), \ i=1, \, 2.
\end{equation}

Comparing the second terms of (\ref{match_discs}), (\ref{match_in_1})  and (\ref{match_in_2}), we find that
\begin{equation}\label{phi_def}
\varphi_1^{(i)}(x) = \alpha_3^{(i)}  \partial_{x_2} u_0^+(x), \quad x \in Q_0^{(0)}, \ x_2 = \varepsilon(j+b_i), \ i=1, \, 2,
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{conj_j_2}
\begin{array}{rcll}
(1 - \eta)  h_1^{-1}(d_0) \partial_r u_0^+(x)&=& \partial_r u_0^{1,-}(x),& x \in Q_0^{(0)} , \ x_2 = \varepsilon(j+b_1)\\[2mm]
\eta h_2^{-1}(d_0) \partial_r u_0^+(x) &=& \partial_r u_0^{2,-}(x), & x \in Q_0^{(0)}, \ x_2 = \varepsilon(j+b_2).
\end{array}
\end{equation}


Since the points $\{ \varepsilon(j+b_i) : \ j=\overline{0, N-1}\}$, $i=1, \, 2,$ make up the $\varepsilon$-net of the segment $[0, \, l]$, we can extend equalities (\ref{with_phi}), (\ref{inD1}), (\ref{inD2}) in  domains $D_i$, equalities (\ref{inQ1}), (\ref{inQ2})  in $Q_0^{(1)}$ and $Q_0^{(2)}$, respectively, and equalities (\ref{conjugation1}), (\ref{phi_def}) and (\ref{conj_j_2})  in $Q_0^{(0)}$. As a result, from equalities (\ref{conj_j_2}) we derive the relation
$$
\eta(x_2)= \frac{h_2(d_0)  \partial_r u_0^{2,-}|_{r=d_0}}{h_1(d_0)  \partial_r u_0^{1,-}|_{r=d_0} + h_2(d_0)  \partial_r u_0^{2,-}|_{r=d_0}}, \quad x_2 \in (0, \, l),
$$
and obtain
$$
\partial_r u_0^+ = h_1(d_0)  \partial_r u_0^{1,-} + h_2(d_0)  \partial_r u_0^{2,-}, \quad x \in Q_0^{(0)}.
$$

By virtue of (\ref{conjugation1}) and (\ref{phi_def}) we can define $\varphi_1^{(i)}$ as follows:
$$
\varphi_1^{(i)}(x) = \alpha_3^{(i)}  \partial_{x_2} u_0^{i,-}(x), \quad x \in D_i, \ i=1, \, 2.
$$

\subsection{The Homogenized Problem}\label{SECTION_LIMIT_PROBLEM_1}

With the help of the first terms $u_0^+$, $u_0^{1,-}$ and $u_0^{2,-}$ of   asymptotic expansions (\ref{exp_out_cyl}) and (\ref{exp_out_discs}) we define multi-sheeted function
$$
{\bf U}_0(x) =
\left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
u_0^+(x), & x \in \Omega_0, \\
u_0^{1,-}(x), & x \in D_1 , \\
u_0^{2,-}(x), & x \in D_2 ,
\end{array}
\right.
$$
or in a short form ${\bf U}_0 = (u_0^+, \, u_0^{1,-}, \, u_0^{2,-})$.
It follows from the foregoing that the components of function ${\bf U}_0$ must satisfy the relations
\begin{equation}\label{res_3}
\left\{
\begin{array}{rcll}
-\Delta u_0^+ + \vartheta_0(u_0^+)&=&f_0
            &\mbox{in} \ \Omega_0,
\\[2mm]
\partial_{x_2}^p u_0^+|_{S^-}&=&\partial_{x_2}^p u_0^+|_{S^+}, & p=0, \, 1,
\\[4mm]
- {\rm div}_{\tilde{x}} (h_1(r)  \nabla_{\tilde{x}} u_0^{1,-}) + h_1(r) \vartheta_0(u_0^{1,-})&&\\
+ 2 \vartheta_1 (u_0^{1,-}) &=&h_1(r) f_0 + 2  \delta_{\beta,1} g_0
            &\mbox{in} \ D_1,
\\[2mm]
\partial_{\nu} u_0^{1,-} + \vartheta_1 (u_0^{1,-})&=& 0
            &\mbox{on} \ Q_0^{(1)},
\\[4mm]
- {\rm
div}_{\tilde{x}} \big( h_2(r) \nabla_{\tilde{x}} u_0^{2,-} \big) + h_2(r) \vartheta_0(u_0^{2,-})&&\\ + 2 \delta_{\alpha,1} \vartheta_2 (u_0^{2,-}) &
=& h_2(r) f_0 + 2 \delta_{\beta,1} g_0
            &\mbox{in} \ D_2,
\\[2mm]
\partial_{\nu} u_0^{2,-} &=& 0
            &\mbox{on} \ Q_0^{(2)},
\\[4mm]
u_0^+|_{Q_0^{(0)}} &=& u_0^{1,-}|_{Q_0^{(0)}} = u_0^{2,-}|_{Q_0^{(0)}},
\\[2mm]
\partial_r u_0^+ &=& h_1(d_0) \partial_r u_0^{1,-} + h_2(d_0) \partial_r u_0^{2,-}
            &\mbox{on} \ Q_0^{(0)}.
\end{array}
\right.
\end{equation}
These relations form {\sl the homogenized problem} for problem (\ref{task1}).

We introduce space ${\cal V}_0 := L^2(\Omega_0)\times L^2(D_1)\times L^2(D_2)$ of multi-sheeted functions with the scalar product
$$
({\bf u} , {\bf v} )_{{\cal V}_0}=\int_{\Omega_0} u_0  v_0
\, dx + \sum_{i=1}^{2} \int_{D_i} u_i v_i \, dx,
$$
where
$$
{\bf u}(x)=
\left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
u_0(x), & x \in \Omega_0,\\
u_1(x), & x \in D_1,\\
u_2(x), & x \in D_2,
\end{array}
\right.
\qquad
\mbox{and}
\qquad
{\bf v}(x)=
\left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
v_0(x), & x \in \Omega_0,\\
v_1(x), & x \in D_1,\\
v_2(x), & x \in D_2,
\end{array}
\right.
$$
or in a short form ${\bf u}=(u_0, \, u_1, \, u_2)$ and ${\bf v}=(v_0, \, v_1, \, v_2)$, belong to ${\cal V}_0$. Also we introduce anisotropic Sobolev space of multi-sheeted functions
\begin{multline*}
{\cal H}_0:= \{ {\bf u} = (u_0 , \, u_1, \, u_2) \in {\cal V}_0: \ \,
u_0 \in H^1(\Omega_0), \ u_0|_{S^-}= u_0|_{S^+}; \\ \exists \, \partial_{x_j} u_i \in L^2(D_i),\ j=1, \, 3, \ i=1, \, 2;\
u_0|_{Q_0^{(0)}} = u_1|_{Q_0^{(0)}} = u_2|_{Q_0^{(0)}} \}
\end{multline*}
with the inner product
$$
({\bf u},{\bf v})_{{\cal H}_0}=
\int_{\Omega_0}(\nabla
                u_0\cdot\nabla v_0 + u_0 v_0) \,dx
+\sum\limits_{i=1}^2\int_{D_i} (
\nabla_{\tilde{x}} u_i  \cdot \nabla_{\tilde{x}} v_i + u_i v_i )\,dx.
$$
It is obvious that ${\cal H}_0$ is continuously embedded in
${\cal V}_0$.

A function ${\bf U}_0 = (u_0^+, \, u_0^{1,-}, \, u_0^{1,-}) \in {\cal H}_0$ is a weak solution to problem (\ref{res_3}) if for any function $\varphi = (\varphi_0, \, \varphi_1, \, \varphi_2) \in {\cal H}_0$ the   integral identity
$$
\int_{\Omega_0}(\nabla
                u_0^+\cdot\nabla \varphi_0 + \vartheta_0(u_0^+) \, \varphi_0) \,dx
+\sum\limits_{i=1}^2\int_{D_i} h_i(r) (
\nabla_{\tilde{x}} u_0^{i,-} \cdot \nabla_{\tilde{x}} \varphi_i  + \vartheta_0(u_0^{i,-}) \, \varphi_i) \, dx
$$ $$
+ 2 \int_{D_1} \vartheta_1(u_0^{1,-}) \, \varphi_1 \, dx + h_1(d_1) \int_{Q_0^{(1)}} \vartheta_1(u_0^{1,-}) \, \varphi_1 \, d\sigma_x + 2 \delta_{\alpha,1} \int_{D_2} \vartheta_2(u_0^{2,-}) \, \varphi_2 \, dx $$
$$
=\int_{\Omega_0} f_0 \varphi_0 \, dx + \sum_{i=1}^2 \int_{D_i} (h_i f_0 + 2 \delta_{\beta,1} g_0) \varphi_i \, dx
$$
holds.

Using the properties of  functions $\vartheta_i$ similarly as in \cite{Mel-m2as-08} we can prove that there exists a unique weak solution to problem~(\ref{res_3}).

\section{Approximation and Asymptotic Estimates}\label{SECTION_ASYMPT_ESTIM}

Let ${\bf U}_0= (u_0^+, \, u_0^{1,-}, \, u_0^{2,-})$ be the unique weak solution to problem (\ref{res_3}). With the help of ${\bf U}_0$ and  solutions $Z_1, \ \Xi_1, \ \Xi_2$ of   junction-layer problems (\ref{junction-layer_Xi}) and (\ref{junction-layer_Z}) we construct the main terms of  expansions (\ref{exp_out_cyl}), (\ref{exp_out_discs}) and (\ref{exp_in}). Consider  smooth cut-off function $\chi_0(r)$, which is equal to 1 as $|r-d_0|<\delta_0/2$ and 0 as $|r-d_0|>\delta_0$, where $\delta_0 \in (0, \, \delta)$ is some fixed number. Matching the outer expansions with the inner expansion with the help of $\chi_0$, we define  approximation function $R_\varepsilon$:
\begin{equation}\label{approx_in_cyl}
R_\varepsilon(x) := R_\varepsilon^+(x)  = u_0^+(x) + \varepsilon \, \chi_0(r) {\cal N}^+(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta), \quad x \in \Omega_0,
\end{equation}
$$
R_\varepsilon(x) := R_\varepsilon^{i,-}(x)=u_0^{i,-}(x) + \varepsilon \left( \tilde{Y}_i\left(\frac{x_2}{\varepsilon}\right) \partial_{x_2} u_0^{i,-}(x) +  \chi_0(r) {\cal N}^{i,-}(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta)\right),
$$
\begin{equation}\label{approx_in_G(j)}
 x \in G_\varepsilon^{(i)}(j), \quad i=1, \, 2.
\end{equation}
Here
$$
{\cal N}^+(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta) = Z_1(\xi) \partial_{x_2} u_0^+|_{r=d_0} + \big(\xi_1 - \eta(x_2) \Xi_1(\xi) - (1 - \eta(x_2))  \Xi_2(\xi)\big)  \partial_r u_0^+|_{r=d_0},
$$
\begin{multline*}
{\cal N}^{i,-} (\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta) = (Z_1(\xi) - \tilde{Y}_i(\xi_2))  \partial_{x_2} u_0^+|_{r=d_0}
\\
+ \big( {\cal Y}_i(\xi_1, \, x_2)  - \eta(x_2)  \Xi_1(\xi) - (1 - \eta(x_2))  \Xi_2(\xi) \big)  \partial_r u_0^+|_{r=d_0},
\end{multline*}
where $\tilde{Y}_i(s):= -s + [s] + b_i + \alpha_3^{(i)}, \ [s]$ is the integer part of $s \in \mathbb{R}, \ i=1, \, 2$, and
$$
\begin{array}{rcl}
{\cal Y}_1(\xi_1, \, x_2) &:=& h_1^{-1}(d_0) \xi_1 (1- \eta(x_2)), \\
{\cal Y}_2(\xi_1, \, x_2)&:=& h_2^{-1}(d_0) \xi_1 \eta(x_2),
\end{array} \quad \xi_1 \le 0, \ x_2 \in (0, \, l).
$$
Obviously, $R_\varepsilon \in H_\varepsilon$.


\begin{theorem}\label{theor1}
Let $f_0 \in H^3(\Omega_1), \ \partial^p_{x_2} f_0|_{S^-} = \partial^p_{x_2} f_0|_{S^+}, \ p=0, \, 1, \ g_0 \in H^1(D_1)$.

Then for any $\mu > 0$ there exist positive constants $\varepsilon_0, \ c_0$ such that for all $\varepsilon \in (0, \, \varepsilon_0)$ the difference between   solution $u_\varepsilon$ to problem (\ref{task1}) and   approximation function $R_\varepsilon$ defined by (\ref{approx_in_cyl}) and (\ref{approx_in_G(j)}), where ${\bf U}_0 = ( u_0^+, \, u_0^{1,-}, \, u_0^{2,-})$ is a weak solution to problem~(\ref{res_3}), satisfies the inequality
\begin{equation}\label{Result3}
\| u_\varepsilon - R_\varepsilon \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)} \le c_0 \, ( \| f_\varepsilon - f_0 \|_{L^2(\Omega_\varepsilon)} + \varepsilon^{1-\mu} + \varepsilon^{\delta_{\alpha,1} (2 - \alpha) + \alpha - 1}  + \varepsilon^{\beta-1} \| g_\varepsilon - g_0 \|_{L^2(D_1)}^{\delta_{\beta,1}}).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}\textbf{Discrepancies in domain $\Omega_0$.} It follows from the first two relations in (\ref{res_3}) and from the theorem's assumptions that $\partial^2_{x_2 x_2} u_0^+|_{S^-} = \partial^2_{x_2 x_2} u_0^+|_{S^+}$. Then, according to the properties of     $Z_1, \ \Xi_1, \ \Xi_2$ and $u_0^+$, function $R_\varepsilon^+$ satisfies   the boundary conditions of problem (\ref{task1}) on $\partial \Omega_\varepsilon \cap \partial \Omega_0$.

Problems (\ref{junction-layer_Xi}) and (\ref{junction-layer_Z}) imply
\begin{equation}\label{deltaN}
\Delta_\xi {\cal N}^+=0, \quad \Delta_\xi {\cal N}^{i,-}=0 \qquad   \xi \in \Pi, \   x_2 \in (0, \, l), \   \theta \in [0, \, 2 \pi], \ i=1, \, 2.
\end{equation}
Let us consider the obvious equality
\begin{equation}\label{komutator}
\Delta_{\tilde{x}} (\chi_0(r) {\cal N}) = \mbox{div}_{\tilde{x}} ({\cal N} \nabla_{\tilde{x}} \chi_0(r)) + \nabla_{\tilde{x}} \chi_0(r) \cdot \nabla_{\tilde{x}} {\cal N} + \chi_0(r) \Delta_{\tilde{x}} {\cal N}, \qquad {\cal N} = {\cal N}(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta).
\end{equation}
Using (\ref{Laplace}), (\ref{res_3}), (\ref{deltaN}) and (\ref{komutator}), we get
$$
-\Delta R_\varepsilon^+(x) - f_\varepsilon(x)
= f_0(x) - f_\varepsilon(x)
- \vartheta_0(u_0^+(x)) +
\chi_0(r) ( r^{-1} \partial_{\xi_1} {\cal N}^+(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta)
$$
$$
- 2 \partial^2_{\xi_2 x_2} {\cal N}^+(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta))
-\varepsilon \, \textrm{div}_{\tilde{x}} ({\cal N}^+|_{\xi_1 = -(r-d_0)/\varepsilon} \nabla_{\tilde{x}} \chi_0(r)) + \chi_0'(r) \partial_{\xi_1} {\cal N}^+(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta)
$$
\begin{equation}\label{wow_body}
-\varepsilon \chi_0(r) \partial^2_{x_2 x_2} {\cal N}^+(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta) - \varepsilon \, r^{-2} \chi_0(r) \, \partial^2_{\theta \theta} {\cal N}^+(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta), \quad x \in \Omega_0.
\end{equation}
We multiply (\ref{wow_body}) by a test function $\psi \in H_\varepsilon$, integrate by parts in $\Omega_0$ and take into account the boundary conditions, satisfied by $R_\varepsilon^+$. This yields
\begin{equation}\label{discrep_in_Omega0}
\int_{\Omega_0} ( \nabla R_\varepsilon^+ \cdot \nabla \psi + \vartheta_0(R_\varepsilon^+) \psi)  \, dx - \int_{\Theta_\varepsilon} \partial_r R_\varepsilon^+ \psi \, d\sigma_x
- \int_{\Omega_0} f_\varepsilon  \psi \, dx
= I_0^+(\varepsilon, \, \psi) + \ldots + I_4^+(\varepsilon, \, \psi),
\end{equation}
where
$$
\begin{array}{rcl}
{ \displaystyle I_0^+(\varepsilon, \, \psi) } &:=& { \displaystyle \int_{\Omega_0} (f_0 - f_\varepsilon)  \psi \, dx ,}
\\[4mm]
{\displaystyle I_1^+(\varepsilon, \, \psi) }&:=& {\displaystyle   \int_{\Omega_0} (\vartheta_0(R^+_\varepsilon) - \vartheta_0 (u_0^+)) \psi \, dx,}
\\[4mm]
{ \displaystyle I_2^+(\varepsilon, \, \psi) }&:=& {\displaystyle   \int_{\Omega_0} \chi_0 (r^{-1} \partial_{\xi_1} {\cal N}^+ -  \partial_{x_2 \xi_2}^2 {\cal N}^+)  \psi \, dx,}
\\[4mm]
{\displaystyle I_3^+(\varepsilon, \, \psi) } &:=& {\displaystyle   \varepsilon \int_{\Omega_0} {\cal N}^+ \nabla_{\tilde{x}} \chi_0 \cdot \nabla_{\tilde{x}} \psi \, dx + \int_{\Omega_0} \chi_0'  \partial_{\xi_1} {\cal N}^+  \psi \, dx ,}
\\[4mm]
{\displaystyle I_4^+(\varepsilon, \, \psi) } &:=& {\displaystyle \varepsilon  \int_{\Omega_0} \chi_0 \partial_{x_2} {\cal N}^+  \partial_{x_2} \psi \, dx + \varepsilon \int_{\Omega_0}  r^{-2}  \chi_0  \partial_\theta {\cal N}^+ \partial_\theta \psi \, dx.}
\end{array}
$$

\textbf{Discrepancies in the thin discs.} One can readily check that
\begin{equation}\label{approx_on_Q_varepsiloni}
\begin{array}{rcll}
\partial_r R_\varepsilon^{1,-} & =& -\vartheta_1(u_0^{1,-}) - \varepsilon \tilde Y_1 \left(\displaystyle \frac{x_2}{\varepsilon} \right) \partial_{x_2} \vartheta_1(u_0^{1,-}), & x \in Q_\varepsilon^{(1)},
\\
\partial_r R_\varepsilon^{2,-} &= &0, & x \in Q_\varepsilon^{(2)},
\end{array}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{approx_on_Theta}
\partial_r R_\varepsilon^{i,-} = \varepsilon  \tilde{Y}_i\left(\frac{x_2}{\varepsilon}\right) \partial_{r x_2}^2 u_0^{i,-} + \partial_r R_\varepsilon^+, \quad x \in \Theta_\varepsilon^{(i)}, \ i=1, \, 2.
\end{equation}
Taking into account (\ref{normal}) and that   functions $h_i$ are constant in a neighborhood of $d_0$, we derive that
\begin{multline}\label{approx_on_S_varepsilon}
\partial_\nu R_\varepsilon^{i,-} = \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt{1 + 4^{-1} \varepsilon^2 |h_i'(r)|^2}} \left(
\pm \tilde{Y}_i\left(\frac{x_2}{\varepsilon}\right) \partial_{x_2 x_2}^2 u_0^{i,-} \pm \chi_0 \frac{\partial}{\partial x_2} ({\cal N}^{i,-}|_{\xi_2=x_2/\varepsilon})  \right.
\\
\left.
- \frac{1}{2} \nabla_{\tilde{x}} h_i \cdot \nabla_{\tilde{x}} (u_0^{i,-} + \varepsilon \tilde{Y}_i \left(\frac{x_2}{\varepsilon}\right) \partial_{x_2} u_0^{i,-})
\right), \quad x \in S_\varepsilon^{(i)}, \ i=1, \, 2,
\end{multline}
where "$+$" \  and "$-$" \ refer to the left and the right parts of the lateral surfaces of the thin discs, respectively.

Relations (\ref{Laplace}), (\ref{res_3}), (\ref{deltaN}) and (\ref{komutator}) yield
$$
-\Delta R_\varepsilon^{i,-}(x) - f_\varepsilon(x)= f_0(x) -f_\varepsilon(x)
- \vartheta_0(u_0^{i,-})
$$
$$
+\chi_0(r) ( r^{-1} \partial_{\xi_1} {\cal N}^{i,-}(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta)
- 2 \partial_{\xi_2 x_2}^2 {\cal N}^{i,-}(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta))
- \varepsilon \textrm{div}_{\tilde{x}} ({\cal N}^{i,-}|_{\xi_1 = - (r- d_0)/\varepsilon}  \nabla_{\tilde{x}} \chi_0(r))
$$
$$
+ \chi_0'(r) \partial_{\xi_1} {\cal N}^{i,-}(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta)
- \varepsilon \chi_0(r) \partial_{x_2 x_2}^2 {\cal N}^{i,-}(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta)
-\varepsilon \chi_0(r) r^{-2} \partial_{\theta \theta}^2 {\cal N}^{i,-}(\xi, \, x_2, \, \theta)
$$
$$
+ \nabla_{\tilde{x}} (\ln h_i(r)) \cdot \nabla_{\tilde{x}} u_0^{i,-}
- \varepsilon \textrm{div} \big( \tilde{Y}_i\left(\frac{x_2}{\varepsilon}\right)  \nabla (\partial_{x_2} u_0^{i,-})\big)
$$
\begin{equation}\label{approx_in_G_before_integrating}
- 2 (1-\delta_{i,2}( 1-\delta_{\alpha,1})) h_i^{-1}(r) \vartheta_i(u_0^{i,-}) + 2 \delta_{\beta,1} h_i^{-1}(r) g_0(x), \quad x \in G_\varepsilon^{(i)}.
\end{equation}

Consider the integral identity
\begin{equation}\label{Sequal}
\int_{S_{\varepsilon}^{(i)}} \frac{\varepsilon h_i(r)}{2
\sqrt{1 + 4^{-1} \varepsilon^2 |h_i'(r)|^2}} \, \varphi \,d\sigma_x =
\int_{G_{\varepsilon}^{(i)}} \varphi \,dx - \varepsilon
\int_{G_{\varepsilon}^{(i)}} Y_i \left( \frac{x_2}{\varepsilon}
\right) \, \partial_{x_2} \varphi \,dx, \qquad i=1, \, 2,
\end{equation}
where $Y_i(s)=-s+[s]+b_i$ and $[s]$ is the integer part of $s$, $\varphi \in
H^1(G_\varepsilon^{(i)})$ is an arbitrary function. We multiply (\ref{approx_in_G_before_integrating}) by a test function $\psi \in H_\varepsilon$ and integrate by parts in $G_\varepsilon^{(i)}$, using (\ref{Sequal}) and taking into account  relations (\ref{approx_on_Q_varepsiloni}), (\ref{approx_on_Theta}), (\ref{approx_on_S_varepsilon}). This yields
\begin{multline}\label{discrep_in_G1}
\int_{G_\varepsilon^{(1)}}  (\nabla R_\varepsilon^{1,-} \cdot \nabla \psi + \vartheta_0( R_\varepsilon^{1,-}) \psi ) \, dx + \varepsilon \int_{S_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \vartheta_1(R_\varepsilon^{1,-})  \psi \, d\sigma_x
+ \int_{Q_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \vartheta_1(R_\varepsilon^{1,-})  \psi \, d\sigma_x
\\
+\int_{\Theta_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \partial_r R_\varepsilon^+ \psi \, d\sigma_x
- \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(1)}} f_\varepsilon  \psi \, dx - \varepsilon^\beta \int_{S_\varepsilon^{(1)}} g_\varepsilon  \psi \, d\sigma_x
= I_0^{1,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi) + \ldots + I_7^{1,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi)
\end{multline}
and
\begin{multline}\label{discrep_in_G2}
\int_{G_\varepsilon^{(2)}}  ( \nabla R_\varepsilon^{2,-} \cdot \nabla \psi  + \vartheta_0( R_\varepsilon^{1,-}) \psi ) \, dx + \varepsilon^\alpha \int_{\Upsilon_\varepsilon^{(2)}} \vartheta_2(R_\varepsilon^{2,-})  \psi \, d\sigma_x
+ \int_{\Theta_\varepsilon^{(2)}} \partial_r R_\varepsilon^+ \psi \, d\sigma_x
\\
- \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(2)}} f_\varepsilon \psi \, dx - \varepsilon^\beta \int_{\Upsilon_\varepsilon^{(2)}} g_\varepsilon \psi \, d\sigma_x
= I_0^{2,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi) + \ldots + I_7^{2,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi)
\end{multline}
for all $\psi \in H_\varepsilon$, where
\begin{flushleft}
${\displaystyle  I_0^{i,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi) := \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(i)}} (f_0 - f_\varepsilon)  \psi \, dx ,}$ \\
${\displaystyle I_1^{i,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi) :=  \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(i)}} ( \vartheta_0(R_\varepsilon^{i,-}) - \vartheta_0(u_0^{i,-}) ) \psi \, dx, }$
${\displaystyle  I_2^{i,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi) :=  \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(i)}} \chi_0  ( r^{-1} \partial_{\xi_1} {\cal N}^{i,-} -  \partial_{x_2 \xi_2}^2 {\cal N}^{i,-} ) \psi \, dx,}$ \\
${\displaystyle  I_3^{i,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi) :=   \varepsilon \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(i)}} {\cal N}^{i,-} \nabla_{\tilde{x}} \chi_0 \cdot \nabla_{\tilde{x}} \psi \, dx   + \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(i)}} \chi_0' \partial_{\xi_1} {\cal N}^{i,-} \psi \, dx ,}$ \\
${\displaystyle  I_4^{i,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi) := \varepsilon \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(i)}} \chi_0 \partial_{x_2} {\cal N}^{i,-} \partial_{x_2} \psi \, dx +    \varepsilon \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(i)}} r^{-2} \chi_0 \partial_\theta {\cal N}^{i,-} \partial_\theta \psi \, dx,}$ \\
${\displaystyle  I_5^{i,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi) := \varepsilon \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(i)}} Y_i\left( \frac{x_2}{\varepsilon} \right) \partial_{x_2} (\psi  \nabla_{\tilde{x}} u_0^{i,-} \cdot \nabla_{\tilde{x}} \ln h_i) \, dx}$
$$
{\displaystyle  +  \varepsilon \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(i)}} \tilde{Y}_i \left( \frac{x_2}{\varepsilon} \right) \nabla (\partial_{x_2} u_0^{i,-}) \cdot \nabla \psi \, dx  , \qquad i=1, \, 2,}
$$
${\displaystyle  I_6^{1,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi) := - \varepsilon \int_{S_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \frac{\vartheta_1(u_0^{1,-})   \psi}{\sqrt{1 + 4^{-1} \varepsilon^2 |h_1'(r)|^2}} \, d\sigma_x -  2 \varepsilon  \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(1)}} Y_1\left(\frac{x_2}{\varepsilon} \right)  h_1^{-1} \partial_{x_2} (\vartheta_1(u_0^{1,-}) \psi) \, dx}$
$$
+ \varepsilon \int_{S_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \vartheta_1(R_\varepsilon^{1,-}) \psi  \, d\sigma_x + \int_{Q_\varepsilon^{(1)}} (\vartheta_1(R_\varepsilon^{1,-}) - \vartheta_1(u_0^{1,-}) - \varepsilon \tilde{Y}_1\left(\frac{x_2}{\varepsilon}\right) \partial_{x_2} \vartheta_1(u_0^{1,-})) \psi \, d\sigma_x,
$$
${\displaystyle  I_6^{2,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi) := - \varepsilon   \delta_{\alpha,1}  \int_{S_\varepsilon^{(2)}} \frac{ \vartheta_2(u_0^{2,-})  \psi}{\sqrt{1 + 4^{-1} \varepsilon^2 |h_2'(r)|^2}} \, d\sigma_x }$
$$
- 2  \varepsilon  \delta_{\alpha,1}  \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(2)}} Y_2\left(\frac{x_2}{\varepsilon} \right)  h_2^{-1} \partial_{x_2} (\vartheta_2(u_0^{2,-}) \psi) \, dx
+ \varepsilon^\alpha \int_{\Upsilon_\varepsilon^{(2)}} \vartheta_2(R_\varepsilon^{2,-}) \psi \, d\sigma_x,
$$
${\displaystyle  I_7^{1,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi) :=  \varepsilon  \delta_{\beta,1} \int_{S_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \frac{g_0  \psi}{\sqrt{1 + 4^{-1} \varepsilon^2 |h_1'(r)|^2}} \, d\sigma_x }$
$$
+ 2  \varepsilon  \delta_{\beta,1} \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(1)}} Y_1 \left( \frac{x_2}{\varepsilon} \right)  h_1^{-1}   \partial_{x_2} (g_0   \psi) \, dx - \varepsilon^\beta \int_{S_\varepsilon^{(1)}} g_\varepsilon  \psi \, d\sigma_x,
$$
${\displaystyle  I_7^{2,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi) := \varepsilon   \delta_{\beta,1} \int_{S_\varepsilon^{(2)}} \frac{g_0  \psi}{\sqrt{1 + 4^{-1} \varepsilon^2 |h_2'(r)|^2}} \, d\sigma_x}$
$$
+ 2  \varepsilon   \delta_{\beta,1} \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(2)}} Y_2 \left( \frac{x_2}{\varepsilon} \right)   h_2^{-1}  \partial_{x_2} (g_0  \psi) \, dx - \varepsilon^\beta \int_{\Upsilon_\varepsilon^{(2)}} g_\varepsilon   \psi \, d\sigma_x.
$$
\end{flushleft}

\textbf{Asymptotic estimates.} After summing (\ref{discrep_in_Omega0}), (\ref{discrep_in_G1}) and (\ref{discrep_in_G2}) we see that   function $R_\varepsilon$ defined by (\ref{approx_in_cyl}) and (\ref{approx_in_G(j)}) satisfies the integral identity
\begin{multline}\label{temp}
\int_{\Omega_\varepsilon} ( \nabla R_\varepsilon \cdot \nabla \psi + \vartheta_0(R_\varepsilon) \psi )\, dx + \varepsilon \int_{S_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \vartheta_1(R_\varepsilon) \psi \, d\sigma_x
+ \int_{Q_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \vartheta_1(R_\varepsilon) \psi \, d\sigma_x
\\
+ \varepsilon^\alpha \int_{\Upsilon_\varepsilon^{(2)}}
\vartheta_2(R_\varepsilon) \psi \, d\sigma_x
-\int_{\Omega_\varepsilon} f_\varepsilon  \psi \, dx - \varepsilon^\beta \int_{S_\varepsilon^{(1)} \cup \Upsilon_\varepsilon^{(2)}} g_\varepsilon  \psi \, d\sigma_x
= F_\varepsilon(\psi)
\end{multline}
for any $\psi \in H_\varepsilon$, where $F_\varepsilon(\psi) := I_0^\pm + \ldots + I_4^\pm + I_5^- + I_6^- + I_7^-$, $I_k^- := I_k^{1,-} + I_k^{2,-}, \ k=\overline{0,7}, \ I_m^\pm := I_m^+ + I_m^-$, $m=\overline{0,4}$.

It follows from (\ref{weakdef}) and (\ref{temp}) that
\begin{multline}\label{corrector_identity}
\int_{\Omega_\varepsilon} ( \nabla (R_\varepsilon - u_\varepsilon) \cdot \nabla \psi  + (\vartheta_0(R_\varepsilon) - \vartheta_0(u_\varepsilon)) \psi) \, dx
+ \varepsilon \int_{S_\varepsilon^{(1)}} (\vartheta_1(R_\varepsilon) - \vartheta_1(u_\varepsilon)) \psi \, d\sigma_x
\\
+ \int_{Q_\varepsilon^{(1)}} (\vartheta_1(R_\varepsilon) - \vartheta_1(u_\varepsilon)) \psi \, d\sigma_x
+ \varepsilon^\alpha \int_{\Upsilon_\varepsilon^{(2)}} (\vartheta_2(R_\varepsilon) - \vartheta_2(u_\varepsilon)) \psi \, d\sigma_x = F_\varepsilon(\psi)
\end{multline}
for all $\psi \in H_\varepsilon.$

Now we are going to estimate $F_\varepsilon(\psi)$.

With the help of Cauchy-Schwartz-Bunyakovskii inequality we obtain
$$
|I_0^\pm(\varepsilon, \, \psi)| \le \| f_\varepsilon - f_0 \|_{L^2(\Omega_\varepsilon )}  \; \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}.
$$

\begin{remark}
Here and further all constants $c_i, \, C_i$ in asymptotic estimates are independent of $\varepsilon$.
\end{remark}

With the help of (\ref{nonlin_condin}), Taylor's formula and Cauchy-Schwartz-Bunyakovskii inequality we derive that
$$
|I_1^+(\varepsilon, \, \psi)| =
\varepsilon \left|\int_{\Omega_0} \vartheta_0'( \, \cdot \,) \chi_0 {\cal N}^+ \psi \, dx \right| \le \varepsilon c_0 \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}.
$$
Similarly we estimate $I_1^-$. Thus, $|I_1^\pm(\varepsilon, \, \psi)| \le \varepsilon C_1 \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}.$

Since   functions $\partial_{\xi_1} {\cal N}^+, \ \partial_{x_2 \xi_2}^2 {\cal N}^+, \ \partial_{\xi_1} {\cal N}^{i,-}, \ \partial_{x_2 \xi_2}^2 {\cal N}^{i,-}$ exponentially decrease as $|\xi_1| \to \infty$ (see (\ref{xi_asymt_1}), (\ref{xi_asymt_2}) and (\ref{z_asympt})), then from Lemma 3.1 in \cite{DeMaio-Melnyk} we derive that
$$
\forall \mu > 0 \quad \exists C_2> 0 \quad \exists \varepsilon_0 > 0 \quad \forall \varepsilon \in (0,\, \varepsilon_0): \quad | I_2^\pm (\varepsilon, \, \psi) | \le \varepsilon^{1-\mu} C_2  \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}.
$$

The integrals in $I_3^\pm(\varepsilon, \, \psi)$ are in fact over
$$
\mbox{supp}(\chi_0'(r)) \cap \Omega_\varepsilon = \{ x \in \Omega_\varepsilon: \ \, \delta_0/2 < |r- d_0| < \delta_0 \},
$$
where, according to (\ref{junction-layer_Xi}) and (\ref{junction-layer_Z}),   functions ${\cal N}^+, \ \partial_{\xi_1} {\cal N}^+, \ \partial_{\xi_1} {\cal N}^{i,-}$ are exponentially small, and   function ${\cal N}^{i,-}$ can be estimated by some constant $c_1$. Thus,
$$
| I_3^\pm(\varepsilon, \, \psi) | \le \varepsilon C_3  \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}.
$$

The integrals in $I_4^\pm$ are over $\{ x\in \mathbb{R}^3: \  |r - d_0| < \delta_0 \}$ and they can be estimated, extracting if necessary the exponentially decreasing part in the corresponding integrand and then using Cauchy-Schwartz-Bunyakovskii inequality. Consider, for example, the integral
$$
\left| \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \chi_0  \partial_{x_2} {\cal N}^{1,-} \partial_{x_2} \psi \, dx \right| =
\left| \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \chi_0   \left( ( Z_1 - \tilde{Y}_1) \partial_{x_2 x_2}^2 u_0^+|_{r=d_0}  \right.\right.
$$
$$- (h_1^{-1}(d_0) \xi_1 + \Xi_1 - \Xi_2) \eta' \partial_r u_0^+|_{r=d_0}
$$
$$
\left. \vphantom{\int_{G_\varepsilon^{(1)}}} \left.+(h_1^{-1}(d_0) \xi_1 (1-\eta)  - \eta \Xi_1 - (1-\eta) \Xi_2 ) \partial_{x_2 r}^2 u_0^+|_{r=d_0} \vphantom{\tilde{Y}_1} \right) \partial_{x_2} \psi \, dx \right|
$$
$$
\le c_2 \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)} \left( \sqrt{\int_0^T \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \chi_0 |Z_1 - \tilde{Y}_1|^2 \, dx dt}
\right.
$$
$$
+\| \alpha_1^{(1)} \eta  + \alpha_2^{(1)} (1 - \eta) +(\alpha_1^{(1)} -\alpha_2^{(1)})\eta'\|_{L^2(G_\varepsilon^{(1)})}
$$
$$
+ \sqrt{ \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \chi_0 \big|h_1^{-1}(d_0) \xi_1 + (\Xi_1-\alpha_1^{(1)}) - (\Xi_2  - \alpha_2^{(1)})\big|^2 \,  dx }
$$
$$
\left.+
\sqrt{ \int_{G_\varepsilon^{(1)}} \chi_0 \big| \eta (\Xi_1-\alpha_1^{(1)}) + (1- \eta) (\Xi_2 - h_1^{-1}(d_0) \xi_1 -\alpha_2^{(1)})  \big|^2 \, dx } \,
\right)
$$
$$
\le c_3 \, \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)} \left( \sqrt{2 \pi l d_0  \varepsilon} \| Z_1 - \tilde{Y}_1 \|_{L^2(\Pi_1^-)}
+ \sqrt{|G_\varepsilon^{(1)}|}\right.
$$
$$
+ \sqrt{2 \pi l d_0  \varepsilon} \, \| h_1^{-1}(d_0) \xi_1 + (\Xi_1-\alpha_1^{(1)}) - (\Xi_2  - \alpha_2^{(1)}) \|_{L^2(\Pi_1^-)}
$$
$$
\left.+ \sqrt{2 \pi l d_0  \varepsilon} \, \| \eta (\Xi_1-\alpha_1^{(1)}) + (1- \eta) (\Xi_2 - h_1^{-1}(d_0) \xi_1 -\alpha_2^{(1)}) \|_{L^2(\Pi_1^-)} \vphantom{ \sqrt{|G_\varepsilon^{(1)}|}}\right),
$$
where $|G_\varepsilon^{(1)}|$ is the measure of $G_\varepsilon^{(1)}$. Relations (\ref{xi_asymt_1}), (\ref{xi_asymt_2}) and (\ref{z_asympt}) show that the norms in the right-hand side of the last inequality are bounded in $\varepsilon$. Similarly we can estimate the rest of the integrals in $I_4^\pm(\varepsilon, \, \psi)$. As a result, we obtain
$$
|I_4^\pm (\varepsilon, \, \psi) | \le \varepsilon C_5 \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}.
$$

\begin{remark} Constants  $C_3$ and $C_4$ depend on
$$
\sup_{x \in Q_0^{(0)}, \ t \in (0,\, T)} \left| \frac{\partial^{|\alpha|}}{\partial x_1^{\alpha_1} \partial x_2^{\alpha_2} \partial x_3^{\alpha_3}}  u_0^+(x)\right|, \quad |\alpha| = \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 + \alpha_3 \le 2, \ \alpha_k \ge0, \ k= \overline{1, \, 3}.
$$
Extending   homogenized problem (\ref{res_3}) periodically in $x_2$ through the planes $\{ x \in \mathbb{R}^3: \ x_2 = 0\}$ and $\{ x \in \mathbb{R}^3: \ x_2 = l\}$ and taking into account the assumptions for $f_0$ and $g_0$, by virtue of classical results on the smoothness of solutions to boundary-value problems  we conclude that these quantities are bounded.
\end{remark}

Since $f_0$ is smooth, then $\partial_{x_2} u_0^{i,-} \in H^1(D_i), \ i=1, \, 2.$ Consequently,
$$
|I_5^- (\varepsilon, \, \psi)| \le \varepsilon c_4  \sum_{i=1}^2 (\| u_0^{i,-} \|_{H^1(D_i)} + \| \partial_{x_2} u_0^{i,-} \|_{H^1(D_i)})  \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}
\le \varepsilon C_5 \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)} .
$$

In order to estimate $I_6^-$ we consider   summand $I_6^{2,-}$ when $\alpha=1$. Obviously, the second integral in $I_6^{2,-}$ can be estimated by $\varepsilon  c_5  \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}$. Using Taylor's formula and obvious equality
$$
1 - \frac{1}{a} = \frac{a^2 -1}{a^2+a} \qquad (a^2 + a \ne 0)
$$
we derive that the sum of the first and the third integrals in $I_6^{2,-}$ is equal to
$$
4^{-1} \varepsilon^3 \int_{S_\varepsilon^{(2)}} \frac{|h_2'(r)|^2 \, \vartheta_2(u_0^{2,-}) \psi}{1 + 4^{-1} \varepsilon^2 |h_2'(r)|^2 + \sqrt{1 + 4^{-1} \varepsilon^2 |h_2'(r)|^2}} \, d\sigma_x
$$
$$
\varepsilon^2  \int_{S_\varepsilon^{(2)}} \vartheta_2'(\, \cdot \, ) (\tilde{Y}_2 \left( \frac{x_2}{\varepsilon} \right) \partial_{x_2} u_0^{2,-} + \chi_0  {\cal N}^{2,-})  \psi \, dx
+ \varepsilon \, \int_{Q_\varepsilon^{(2)}} \vartheta_2(R_\varepsilon) \psi \, d\sigma_x
$$
$$
=: J_1(\varepsilon, \, \psi) + J_2(\varepsilon, \, \psi) + J_3(\varepsilon, \, \psi).
$$
With the help of (\ref{nonlin_condin}) and (\ref{Sequal}) we obtain $|J_1(\varepsilon, \, \psi) + J_2(\varepsilon, \, \psi)| \le \varepsilon c_6 \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}$. Taking into account (\ref{nonlin_condin}), properties of the trace operator and the fact that $f_0$ is smooth, we get $|J_3(\varepsilon, \, \psi)| \le \varepsilon c_7 \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}$. Thus, in case $\alpha = 1$ we have
$$
|I_6^{2,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi)| \le \varepsilon c_8 \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}.
$$
In case $\alpha>1$ with the help of (\ref{Sequal}) we obtain $|I_6^{2,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi)| \le \varepsilon^{\alpha-1} c_9 \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}$.

Similarly to $I_6^{2,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi)$, we estimate $I_6^{1,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi)$ and $I_7^-(\varepsilon, \, \psi)$. As a result, we get
$$
|I_6^{1,-}(\varepsilon, \, \psi)| \le \varepsilon C_6 \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}
$$
and
$$
|I_7^-(\varepsilon, \, \psi) | \le C_7 \left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
(\varepsilon + \| g_0 - g_\varepsilon \|_{L^2(D_1)} )  \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}, & \beta =1, \\[2mm]
\varepsilon^{\beta-1} \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)}, & \beta >1.
\end{array} \right.
$$

Thus,
\begin{equation}\label{Finish}
|F_\varepsilon(\psi)| \le C_8 \, ( \| f_\varepsilon - f_0 \|_{L^2(\Omega_\varepsilon)} + \varepsilon^{1-\mu} + \varepsilon^{\delta_{\alpha,1} (2 - \alpha) + \alpha - 1}
+ \varepsilon^{\beta-1} \| g_\varepsilon - g_0 \|_{L^2(D_1 )}^{\delta_{\beta,1}}) \| \psi \|_{H^1(\Omega_\varepsilon)},
\end{equation}
where $\mu > 0$ is an arbitrary number. Setting in (\ref{corrector_identity}) $\psi := R_\varepsilon - u_\varepsilon$ and using (\ref{Finish}) and obvious inequality
$$
(\vartheta_i(s_1) - \vartheta_i(s_2))(s_1 - s_2) \ge c_1 (s_1 - s_2)^2 \qquad \forall s_1, \, s_2 \in \mathbb{R}, \ i=\overline{0,2},
$$
which follows from (\ref{nonlin_condin}), we obtain estimate (\ref{Result3}).
\end{proof}

\section{Discussion of the Obtained Results}\label{SECTION_DISCUSS}

As we can see from the obtained results, the homogenized problem~(\ref{res_3}) for problem~(\ref{task1}) is a nonstandard boundary-value problems for multi-sheeted function ${\bf U}_0$ in anisotropic Sobolev space ${\cal H}_0$ (see Section \ref{SECTION_LIMIT_PROBLEM_1}). This problem consists of three boundary-value problems (in domains $\Omega_0$ and $D_i, \ i=1,\,2$), connected with each other by the conjugation conditions (on $Q_0^{(0)}$).

The nonhomogeneous Robin boundary conditions on the lateral surfaces of the thin discs in problem~(\ref{task1}) are transformed as $\varepsilon \to 0$ into new summands in the differential equations in  domains $D_i, \ i=1,\,2,$ in   problem~(\ref{res_3}). These summands show us the influence of the perturbed parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$. If $\alpha > 1$, then summand $2 \delta_{\alpha,1} \vartheta_1(u_0^{2,-})$ vanishes. From physical point of view this means that the outer heat conduction coefficient is too small, and we can neglect this heat exchange. If $\beta>1$, then summands $2 \delta_{\beta,1} g_0$ vanish, which means that the temperature of the environment is too small, and we can consider it equal to zero.

Also functions $h_i, \ i=1,\,2$, which describe the relative thickness of the thin discs from the $i$-th level, are transformed into the coefficients of the differential equations in   domains $D_i$, respectively. The variable $x_2$ is involved as a parameter in the boundary-value problems in $D_i, \ i=1,\,2$, which shows us the influence of the type of  thick junction $\Omega_\varepsilon$ on the asymptotic behavior of   solution $u_\varepsilon$.

From results proved in the present paper it follows that for applied
problems in thick junctions we can use the  homogenized problem (\ref{res_3}), which is simpler, instead of
the initial problem~(\ref{task1}) with sufficient plausibility.
